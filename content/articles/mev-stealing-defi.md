<p dir="ltr">Last year, you were robbed. You probably didn't notice.</p><p dir="ltr">If you made any trades on a decentralized exchange in 2025, bots extracted value from your transactions. Not through hacking. Not through a bug. Through a feature built into how public blockchains work.</p><p dir="ltr">This is called MEV — Maximal Extractable Value. And in 2025, bots extracted more than $1.5 billion from regular DeFi users.</p><h3 dir="auto">How the robbery works</h3><p dir="ltr">When you submit a transaction to Solana (or Ethereum, or any public blockchain), it doesn't execute immediately. It enters a public queue called the mempool.</p><p dir="ltr">In that brief moment — before your transaction confirms — every bot on the network can see exactly what you're trying to do.</p><p dir="ltr">If you're buying SOL with USDC, a bot sees this. It immediately:</p><ol dir="auto"><li><p>Buys SOL before you, driving the price up slightly</p></li><li><p>Lets your transaction execute at the higher price</p></li><li><p>Sells the SOL it bought for an instant profit</p></li></ol><p dir="ltr">You get worse execution. The bot makes money. This happens in milliseconds, thousands of times per day, on every public chain.</p><h3 dir="auto">Why existing solutions fail</h3><p dir="ltr">Some projects have tried to solve MEV with:</p><ul dir="auto"><li><p><strong>Transaction ordering services</strong>: Delay when transactions are revealed. Still public, just later.</p></li><li><p><strong>ZK proofs</strong>: Hide the sender, not the transaction content. MEV bots can still see the trade.</p></li><li><p><strong>Commit-reveal schemes</strong>: Two-step transactions. Add latency, don't solve the root problem.</p></li></ul><p dir="ltr">None of these fix the fundamental issue: the transaction data is eventually public, and bots are fast enough to exploit any window.</p><h3 dir="auto">The only real solution: encrypt the transaction itself</h3><p dir="ltr">Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) does something remarkable: it lets a computer perform computations on encrypted data without decrypting it first.</p><p dir="ltr">What this means in practice: your swap order can be encrypted, processed encrypted, and executed encrypted. No one — including validators — ever sees the plaintext transaction until it's fully confirmed.</p><p dir="ltr">MEV becomes mathematically impossible because there's nothing to front-run.
